Section 01 · The Visibility Problem
Three-tier visibility.
Only one tier is actually visible.
The exact same structural gap that exists in financial markets — Speed 1 vs Speed 2 vs Speed 3 — exists in supply chains. Tier-1 suppliers are well-monitored. Tier-2 suppliers are barely visible. Tier-3 and beyond are completely dark. But disruptions almost always originate there.

Your direct, named suppliers — the ones on your contracts

Section 04 · Supply Chain Tribal Knowledge
What a 20-year supply chain veteran
knows that no tool captures
The same tribal knowledge problem that exists in financial markets lives inside every corporate supply chain function. Senior SCM leaders carry relationship maps, supplier calibrations, and disruption patterns in their heads. When they leave, so does the institutional memory — and the company's resilience.
01
Single-Source Knowledge
The Hidden Single-Source Dependencies Nobody Documented
Every experienced SCM leader knows 10–20 "silent single-source" dependencies — components that look like they have alternates in the system but in practice cannot be switched without 18 months of requalification. This knowledge is built from requalification failures, quality incidents, and institutional memory of past substitution attempts. It is never systematically captured in ERP or sourcing systems.
"We have three listed alternates for that gasket material. But anyone who's been here 5 years knows none of them qualify for the chemical resistance spec. We're effectively single-sourced and the system doesn't know it."
MKG Bridge
MKG's graph encodes qualification constraints as edge metadata. "Alternate exists but unqualified" becomes a graph property — a distinct edge type from "certified alternate." Graph traversal scores single-source risk correctly, not based on what the system says exists but on what is actually substitutable. Survives leadership departure.
02
Supplier Calibration
Supplier-Specific Behavioral Calibration — "This one always overpromises recovery"
Experienced procurement managers develop precise, company-specific calibration for how suppliers communicate during disruptions. Some suppliers always understate severity to protect the relationship. Others are transparent and accurate. Some consistently recover 30% faster than their initial estimate. Others chronically miss recovery dates. This calibration is never in the supplier scorecard system — it lives in the relationship manager's judgment.
"When that supplier says '4-week recovery,' we plan for 8. Every time, for 12 years. It's not in their scorecard — it's just what everyone on the desk knows. When we get new account managers they always get burned by it the first time."
MKG Bridge
Historical disruption event data per supplier: stated recovery date vs. actual recovery date across all recorded events. Systematic over/under-promise bias becomes a computable calibration factor per supplier entity. New procurement managers inherit the institutional calibration on day one — the pattern survives even complete team turnover.
03
Canary Suppliers
The Canary Suppliers — "This one always signals what's coming for the whole sector"
In every industry, there are small, under-monitored suppliers whose operational changes systematically lead the entire sector. A small specialty chemical company whose order book changes always precede semiconductor lead time shifts by 6 weeks. A specialized logistics provider in a single region whose capacity utilisation signals broader logistics tightening before it becomes visible in rates. Veterans know these canaries. They watch them obsessively. No ERP or SCRM system tracks them as leading indicators.
"There's a specialty substrate supplier in Taiwan — 8 companies in the world make what they make. When their lead times go from 4 weeks to 10 weeks, every automotive OEM's tier-1 chips follow within 6 weeks. I've seen it happen four times."
MKG Bridge
Lead-lag edge detection: edges with consistent temporal precedence (Supplier A's signals precede industry-wide disruptions by N weeks, validated across historical events) are encoded as "canary" edge types with lead-time metadata. The canary relationships become graph queries, discoverable by any analyst — not just the veteran who spent years figuring it out.
04
Relationship Strength
Real Relationship Weight vs. Contracted Volume — What the Relationship Actually Is
In supply chain databases, relationships are binary (supplier or not) or measured by contracted volume. But the real weight of a relationship — the willingness to prioritise your order in a shortage, the goodwill built over 20 years of collaboration, the personal relationship between your CPO and their CEO — is nowhere in any system. During a shortage, this informal relationship strength determines whose orders get filled first. Veterans know exactly which suppliers will "take care of" them when capacity is constrained.
"On paper we're their 8th largest customer. In practice their CEO calls our CPO personally before allocating scarce capacity. That relationship is worth $50M in a shortage. It's not in any database."
MKG Bridge
Multi-dimensional edge weights: contracted volume weight (from procurement systems) + relationship quality signals (from news co-mentions, press release joint references, executive relationship signals from LinkedIn/filings) + historical allocation behavior during past shortages. A composite relationship strength score that captures more than pure volume. Encoded, not just remembered.
05
Disruption Pattern Memory
Historical Disruption Pattern Library — "This exact pattern happened in 2011"
Experienced supply chain leaders carry a mental library of historical disruption patterns. They immediately recognize when a current situation matches a past pattern — and they know exactly how it played out: which suppliers failed first, which alternatives were available, how long recovery took, what the sequence of events was. This pattern recognition drives faster, more accurate response. It is entirely unencoded in any system and completely lost when leaders retire.
"This is almost identical to the Japan earthquake in 2011. Same semiconductor substrate issue, same supplier concentration. In 2011 it took 14 weeks to recover. The recovery sequence went: X first, then Y, then Z. Let me call our Y supplier now before they get overwhelmed."
