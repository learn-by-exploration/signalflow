Vertical A · Financial Markets
Investment Intelligence
for Alpha Generation
The problem: Markets price direct earnings events in milliseconds (Speed 1). They price intra-industry spillovers in hours (Speed 2). But shock propagation through supply chains, board networks, joint ventures, and regulatory co-exposures — confirmed by peer-reviewed research to take 1–14 weeks (Speed 3) — is completely invisible to every commercial financial data platform.

The mechanism: When TSMC reports earnings, every sell-side analyst updates their NVIDIA model within 3 hours. But the 800+ companies in NVIDIA's downstream ecosystem — the GPU-dependent software vendors, the data center cooling suppliers, the Korean PCB makers, the electrical grid operators near AI campuses — are not re-priced for days to weeks. The relationship graph exists. The signals exist. The product to connect them does not.

Why the delay is permanent: Human cognitive limits cannot be removed by faster news delivery. Getting TSMC's earnings faster does not help a PM know which of 58,000 listed companies has an undiscovered second-order dependency on TSMC capacity. Even with AlphaSense surfacing every related document, mapping documents to relationship weights, traversing the network, and scoring downstream impact takes hours to days — market moves while they work. MKG reduces this to 60 seconds. The window does not close for days. That is the exploitable edge.

Academic proof: PEAD (Post-Earnings Announcement Drift) has been documented since 1968. DellaVigna & Pollett demonstrated that on Fridays, 60% of total stock price response to earnings news is delayed — structural investor inattention. The Supply Chain Contagion literature (POM 2024) documents 1–2 week delay for customer→supplier crash risk transmission. The alpha opportunity is not a theory. It is the oldest and most persistent anomaly in capital markets.

Vertical B · Supply Chain
Disruption Intelligence
for Operational Resilience
The problem: Supply chain disruptions cost an average of 8% of annual revenue and occur every 3.7 years on average (McKinsey). 94% of companies report revenue impact. But only 6% have full supply chain visibility — and nearly all visibility stops at Tier 1. Disruptions almost always originate in Tier 2, 3, or beyond: the 2021 automotive chip shortage began at a single Japanese semiconductor plant 3 hops from the automotive OEMs whose production collapsed.

The mechanism: A factory fire in Japan, a regulatory change in China, a Houthi attack in the Red Sea — all are news events that appear in structured and unstructured sources hours to weeks before they reach production lines. But no system reads those signals across a dynamic, multi-tier supplier graph and delivers a ranked impact assessment with affected production platforms, delay estimates, and alternative sourcing options. Companies discover Tier-3 disruptions only when Tier-1 suppliers begin failing — and by then it is too late to mitigate.

Why the delay is permanent: Tier-2 and Tier-3 supplier relationships are not in ERP systems. They are not in SAP Ariba or Coupa. They are in the heads of experienced procurement managers who built their mental maps over 15–20 years — and take those maps with them when they leave. When China published yttrium export restrictions in Chinese-language regulatory filings in April 2025, companies without multilingual monitoring missed 11 days of warning. MKG reads Chinese regulatory filings in real time. There is no language gap.

Regulatory tailwind: EU CSDD (Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive), UK Modern Slavery Act, US CHIPS Act supply chain disclosure, and SEC climate disclosure rules all require continuous multi-tier supply chain monitoring. Regulatory compliance is accelerating the demand for exactly what MKG provides.
