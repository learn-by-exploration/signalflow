Section 01  ·  The Four Knowledge Layers
What tools give. What humans carry.
Where the edge actually lives.
There are four distinct layers of knowledge a portfolio manager brings to every investment decision. Commercial tools serve the top layer brilliantly and the second partially. The bottom two — where the genuine edge lives — are completely unencoded.
Well-Served
📊
Explicit
DOCUMENTED · TRANSFERABLE · QUERYABLE
What is written down. Prices, financial statements, earnings releases, SEC filings, analyst reports, news articles. Unambiguous. Any analyst can retrieve it instantly. Zero interpretation required. Zero edge remaining.
Examples:
TSMC Q4 2025 revenue: $26.9B (+37% YoY). NVIDIA stock price: $145.83 at close. S&P 500 PE ratio: 28.6x.

Examples:
"TSMC's management sounded more confident than usual on pricing power." "NVIDIA's margins are expanding faster than Street consensus."

Section 02  ·  Taxonomy of Financial Tribal Knowledge
The six things a veteran PM
knows that no tool shows
These are not vague intuitions. They are specific, actionable, computable insights — carried verbally in experienced heads, lost on departure, and completely invisible to every commercial financial data platform.
01
The Secret Relationship Map — "These two move together, nobody covers it"
Relationship
Every seasoned sector analyst carries a mental map of undisclosed or under-covered relationship edges. These are not in Capital IQ. They don't appear in Bloomberg relationship databases. They emerge from years of reading earnings call footnotes, noticing pattern co-movement, attending industry conferences, and reading regional press that no data vendor indexes. A veteran semiconductor analyst knows 50 relationships that the database doesn't.
"AMAT always moves 2–3 weeks after ASML results, because their booking lead times are almost identical but AMAT's IR doesn't front-run it the way ASML's does. It's not in any database. You just know it after two cycles."
The problem is structural: Capital IQ records relationships from filings. But the most valuable relationship intelligence comes from patterns not disclosed in filings — operational dependencies revealed only in aggregate market behavior, industry contacts, and historical co-movement. When the analyst who tracked this leaves, the knowledge goes with them.
MKG Bridge Point
Graph construction from co-movement signals + earnings call NLP extracts the implicit relationship structure. Edge weights built from co-mention frequency, lead-lag co-movement, and supply chain filing cross-reference surface relationships that are "known but unwritten" — encoding tribal knowledge into graph edges that survive analyst turnover.
02
Company-Specific Calibration — "This CEO always sandbagged by 15%"
Calibration
Experienced analysts develop precise, company-specific calibration adjustments that are never written in any research note. They know which CEOs systematically underpromise and which overstate. They know which quarters of the year a company consistently surprises in one direction. They know which management teams get nervous under pressure from specific analysts, and what that nervousness signals. Fuller & Thaler built a $3B fund exploiting anchoring bias in analyst estimates — exactly this type of calibration. But it lives entirely in human heads at most funds.
"This CFO always guides down 8–12% from what they actually expect in Q3. It's his style. We adjust for it every single time. It's never written in any note — it's just what the desk knows."
MKG Bridge Point
Historical guidance vs. actuals database per entity per executive. WAN learns systematic over/under-guidance patterns per management team from historical earnings data. Calibration factors become explicit, computable, and survivable beyond individual analyst tenure.
03
The Canary Companies — "Watch this one, nobody else does"
Leading Indicator
In every sector, there are small, under-covered companies whose results systematically lead larger, better-covered companies by weeks. Experienced sector analysts know these canaries intimately. They watch small substrate suppliers before semiconductors, small trucking companies before industrials, obscure chemicals distributors before energy. These are not discoverable by searching Bloomberg. They are found through years of sector immersion and pattern recognition. The research finding from Shanghai lockdown data confirms it: tier-1 suppliers showed significant abnormal returns before focal firms did. The canaries exist. Knowing which ones requires tribal knowledge.
"There's a substrate supplier in Taiwan — 10 analysts cover it globally. When their order intake drops more than 12%, TSMC's advanced packaging revenue misses 6 weeks later. Every cycle, like clockwork. It's not in any database."
MKG Bridge Point
Lead-lag relationship detection across the graph: edges with consistent temporal precedence (Company A moves before Company B by N weeks, validated across N historical events) are automatically surfaced as "leading indicator" edge types. The canaries are encoded as graph edges with directional temporal lag metadata.
04
Relationship Decay Knowledge — "That partnership means less than it used to"
Weight Decay
Relationships change in strength over time, but databases don't update. A veteran analyst knows that a joint venture announced four years ago has become less operationally significant as one party diversified. They know that a supply chain relationship that was critical in 2019 has been partially onshored by 2024. They know that executive relationships that drove a board interloc appointment are no longer active because the executives moved on. Capital IQ still shows all of these as active, with the same implied weight as day one. The analyst's calibrated decay knowledge is entirely unencoded.
"That JV is still in the database. But anyone who followed it knows the two companies barely interact anymore — one of them pivoted their strategy in 2022. The edge weight in any system treating it as 2019-equivalent is simply wrong."
MKG Bridge Point
Temporal edge weight decay: edges with no news reinforcement decay toward zero on a configurable schedule. WAN reads absence of co-mention, filing reference drops, and management departure signals to automatically reduce edge weights of aging relationships — encoding the decay knowledge that currently lives only in experienced analysts' heads.
05
Sector Cycle Timing — "We're at week 6 of a 14-week pattern"
Cycle Timing
Experienced sector analysts develop precise mental models of cycle dynamics that go far beyond what any data system captures. They know the historical sequence of which companies announce first, how the market initially misreads the announcement, which second-order companies reprice with what typical delay, and how long the cycle typically lasts before a correction. This is Pattern Recognition built from witnessing 3–5 full cycles in a sector — typically 10–15 years of sector experience. No data system captures the cycle sequence as a structured, queryable, replayable pattern.
"I've seen four semiconductor upcycles. They all follow the same sequence. We're at week 6 of a typical 14-week propagation — TSMC has reported, ASML has confirmed the orders, now we're waiting for the memory players to inflect. The desk consensus is we're 40% through this particular cycle."
MKG Bridge Point
Temporal graph rewind enables cycle pattern comparison: replay graph state at any historical timestamp, diff two cycle starting points, and identify which edges activated in which sequence. Historical propagation sequences become queryable baselines for current cycle comparison — cycle timing knowledge becomes a graph query, not a PM's memory.
06
Regulatory Co-Exposure — "Both face the same regulator, nobody is writing about it"
Regulatory
Certain companies face the same regulatory body, the same political risk exposure, or the same policy environment — and when scrutiny hits one, it systematically reaches the other. This knowledge is not in any commercial database. It comes from reading regulatory filings, understanding agency jurisdictions, following congressional committee assignments, and tracking which companies have shared lobbying relationships. An experienced DC-focused analyst knows which tech companies share a critical FTC commissioner relationship, which energy companies are on the same EPA permit renewal schedule, which banks have the same internal model validation cycle. This knowledge is almost never documented.
"Both these companies file under the same FERC docket number. Nobody outside DC knows this. When FERC ruled against the first one last month, the second one will follow within 90 days. I've watched this commissioner for six years."
MKG Bridge Point
Regulatory entity nodes in the graph: regulatory bodies, government agencies, and policy instruments become first-class entities with edges to the companies they govern. WAN reads regulatory news, agency announcements, and congressional filings to create co-exposure edges that are currently carried only in specialists' heads.

The Measurement
Investment management has a turnover-driven knowledge destruction cycle. Average PM tenure at a hedge fund is 3–5 years. Senior sector analyst tenure is longer but still ends. Every departure takes the bottom two layers of the iceberg with it. The top two (Bloomberg, FactSet) stay. The edge — the relationship map, the calibrations, the canaries — goes. The successor starts rebuilding from scratch, spending 12–18 months reconstructing what their predecessor had accumulated over a decade. This is a permanent, structural inefficiency at every fund.

The Core Insight
Bloomberg gives you what happened. AlphaSense gives you what was said. RavenPack gives you how people felt about it. FactSet gives you what it's worth.

But the $900K/year tool stack still cannot answer: which other companies does this signal reach, through what relationships, with what weights, in what sequence, with what historical precedent — in the next 60 seconds, automatically, with full explainability?

That answer has lived entirely in experienced humans' heads for 50 years. MKG is the first system designed to encode it.
